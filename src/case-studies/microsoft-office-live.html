Microsoft Office Live Workspaces

A dropbox equivalent for Microsoft Office Information Workers, supporting both WYSIWYG site builder capabilities and collaborative file storage.

Client
Microsoft
Role
UX Designer
Duration
18 Months
Team
6 UX Designers, 2 UX Leads, Technical Fellow

Project Summary

Problem

Office Live served two audiences: a small‑business site builder and a Workspaces file locker; both needed clearer IA and simpler setup.

Approach

Reworked the home shell and "Configurator" for site setup; clarified storage plans, templates, and publishing steps.

Result

Faster time‑to‑publish for SMBs and cleaner Workspaces flows; reduced support friction during launch.

Research

Understanding users, market, and opportunities

Discovery & Analysis

Deep dive into user needs, behaviors, and pain points through comprehensive research methods.

  • User interviews and surveys with Information Workers
  • Competitive analysis of Dropbox and similar file storage solutions
  • Market research for small business website builders
  • Stakeholder interviews with Technical Fellow Suri Raman
  • Analytics review of existing Office Live usage patterns

Key Insights

  • Information Workers needed seamless Office document integration
  • Small businesses struggled with website setup complexity
  • Users wanted familiar Office-like interface patterns
  • Collaboration features were highly valued but underutilized

Design Principles

  • Command Discovery - Make powerful tools visually findable

  • Efficiency - Fewer clicks to accomplish tasks

Lessons Learned

Collaborative Magic

Working with the Technical Fellow was one of the highlights of this project. His ability to envision bold concepts, paired with our collaborative synergy, made the process inspiring and deeply energizing. I thrive when someone brings a strong vision to the table — it's incredibly rewarding to build on that momentum with layered ideas, nuanced features, and thoughtful interaction design.

Pair Programming & Dev Alignment

When late-stage dev complications threatened the release timeline, I jumped into late-night sessions with engineering to work side-by-side in a tight design–build loop. We navigated a dual-track workflow that blended elements of Agile and Waterfall, ensuring pixel-perfect fidelity across layouts, smoothing out key interaction bugs, and rapidly optimizing CSS and frontend user flows. Honestly? It was fun. I love being hands-on and collaborative when things get crunchy.

What I'd Improve

Earlier in the process, we developed presentations and motion prototypes that gained traction across the org — they had legs. I only wish our final vision presentation had been seen more widely. We'd built blue-sky user flows and scenarios that were bold, future-facing, and rooted in community-first thinking, but some of that work was lost during reorg transitions. If I could go back, I'd push harder for early alignment with leadership to ensure those efforts landed with full strategic visibility.
